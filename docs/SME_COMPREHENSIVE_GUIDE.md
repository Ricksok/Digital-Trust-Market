## What is an SME?

**SME** stands for **Small and Medium Enterprises** (also referred to as Small and Medium-sized Enterprises). These are businesses that fall between micro-enterprises and large corporations in terms of size, revenue, and number of employees.

SMEs are the backbone of most economies worldwide, representing a significant portion of businesses and employment. They are characterized by:
- Limited scale of operations
- Local or regional market focus
- Owner-managed structure
- Limited access to formal capital markets
- High innovation potential
- Flexibility and adaptability

## SME Classification and Definitions

### Global Definitions

Different countries and organizations use varying criteria to define SMEs. Common parameters include:
- **Number of employees**
- **Annual revenue/turnover**
- **Total assets**
- **Capital investment**

### Kenyan Definition (According to Micro and Small Enterprises Act, 2012)

In Kenya, SMEs are classified as follows:

#### **Micro Enterprise**
- Employees: 1-9 employees
- Annual turnover: Up to KES 500,000 (~$5,000 USD)
- Assets: Up to KES 5 million (~$50,000 USD)

#### **Small Enterprise**
- Employees: 10-49 employees
- Annual turnover: KES 500,001 - KES 5 million (~$5,000 - $50,000 USD)
- Assets: KES 5 million - KES 20 million (~$50,000 - $200,000 USD)

#### **Medium Enterprise**
- Employees: 50-99 employees
- Annual turnover: KES 5 million - KES 800 million (~$50,000 - $8 million USD)
- Assets: KES 20 million - KES 100 million (~$200,000 - $1 million USD)

### World Bank Classification

- **Small Enterprise**: 5-19 employees
- **Medium Enterprise**: 20-99 employees

### European Union Classification

- **Small Enterprise**: 
  - Employees: < 50
  - Annual turnover: < €10 million OR balance sheet total < €10 million
- **Medium Enterprise**:
  - Employees: < 250
  - Annual turnover: < €50 million OR balance sheet total < €43 million

## Characteristics of SMEs

### 1. **Size and Scale**
- Limited number of employees (typically 10-250)
- Moderate revenue compared to large corporations
- Local or regional market presence
- Limited market share

### 2. **Ownership and Management**
- Often owner-managed or family-owned
- Direct involvement of owners in daily operations
- Less formal organizational structure
- Personal relationships important in business

### 3. **Financial Characteristics**
- Limited capital resources
- Heavy reliance on owner's equity and personal savings
- Limited access to formal credit
- Cash flow challenges
- Limited financial record-keeping

### 4. **Operational Characteristics**
- High flexibility and adaptability
- Quick decision-making
- Limited bureaucracy
- Focus on niche markets
- Innovation potential
- Limited economies of scale

### 5. **Resource Constraints**
- Limited human resources
- Limited financial resources
- Limited access to technology
- Limited marketing budgets
- Limited research and development capacity

### 6. **Market Position**
- Often serve local/regional markets
- Compete with larger corporations
- Price-sensitive customers
- Relationship-based business

## SME Financing Needs

### Types of Financing Needs

#### **1. Working Capital Financing**
- **Purpose**: Day-to-day operations
- **Examples**: Inventory purchase, payroll, utilities
- **Characteristics**: Short-term, recurring, urgent
- **Amount**: Small to medium (KES 50,000 - KES 5 million)

#### **2. Growth Capital**
- **Purpose**: Business expansion
- **Examples**: New equipment, new markets, increased capacity
- **Characteristics**: Medium-term, growth-oriented, strategic
- **Amount**: Medium to large (KES 500,000 - KES 50 million)

#### **3. Asset Financing**
- **Purpose**: Purchase of fixed assets
- **Examples**: Machinery, vehicles, property
- **Characteristics**: Long-term, collateral-backed, specific
- **Amount**: Medium to large (KES 1 million - KES 100 million)

#### **4. Project Financing**
- **Purpose**: Specific projects
- **Examples**: New product launch, market entry, infrastructure
- **Characteristics**: Project-based, time-bound, structured
- **Amount**: Variable (KES 100,000 - KES 100 million)

#### **5. Trade Financing**
- **Purpose**: Trade transactions
- **Examples**: Import/export, supply chain, receivables
- **Characteristics**: Transaction-based, short-term, trade-related
- **Amount**: Small to medium (KES 200,000 - KES 10 million)

### Financing Challenges

#### **1. Collateral Requirements**
- SMEs often lack sufficient collateral
- Banks require fixed assets or guarantees
- Personal guarantees may not be sufficient
- High collateral-to-loan ratios

#### **2. Credit History**
- Limited or no credit history
- Informal operations
- Poor financial records
- Unproven track record

#### **3. High Interest Rates**
- Perceived high risk
- Limited competition
- High cost of funds
- Risk premiums

#### **4. Complex Application Processes**
- Lengthy procedures
- Multiple requirements
- High documentation needs
- Time-consuming approvals

#### **5. Limited Financial Literacy**
- Poor understanding of financial products
- Inadequate financial planning
- Limited business skills
- Poor record-keeping
